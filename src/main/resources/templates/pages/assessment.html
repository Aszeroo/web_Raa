Ransomware Readiness Assessment

Robust Data Backup (DB)

Be prepared to recover gracefully from an incident. Employ a backup solution that automatically and continuously backs up your business-critical data and system configurations. Ensure the backed-up data is stored securely (encrypted) offsite or in the cloud and allows for at least 30 days of rollback. Periodically test your ability to recover data from backup.
DB:B.Q01 Are important systems and data backed up daily to an offsite location with the ability to restore multiple versions back at least 30 days?

Question Intent: To determine if important systems are backed up daily to an offsite location with the ability to restore back at least 30 days.
Learn to protect your information as it is stored, processed, or transmitted. Identify information critical to operations stored on your network. Have plans in place to help recover and restore systems, networks, and data from known good backups. Regular backups protect against ransomware and other forms of malware attacks. Use on-site and remote backup methods to protect vulnerable information. Periodic testing of the organization’s backup and storage procedures ensures continued validity as operational conditions change.
Online and cloud storage backup services can help protect against data loss and provide encryption as an added level of security. Identify key files you need access to if online backups are unavailable when you do not have an internet connection.
Possible actions:
Employ a backup solution that automatically and continuously backs up your business-critical data and system configurations. Leverage protections for backups, including physical security, encryption, and offline copies.
To increase the chances of recovering lost or corrupted data, follow the 3-2-1 rule:
3 – Keep three copies of any important file: one primary and two backups.
2 – Keep the files on two different media types to protect against different types of hazards.
1 – Store one copy – or “go bag” – off-site (e.g., outside the home or business facility).

Criteria for “Yes” Response:
Important systems and data are backed up daily.
And, backed up to an offsite location.
And, created with the ability to restore multiple versions back at least 30 days.

DB:B.Q02 Are data backups tested annually?

Question Intent: To determine if the organization test its data backups at least annually. Creating and restoring backup files can help mitigate the destructive effects of a ransomware attack. Periodically test your ability to recover data from backups to verify the integrity of the backups and their ability to be restored. The more critical or important the data, the more often you should test. Test your backup strategy before you need to use it to make sure you have full read-back verification, a method of preventing errors when information is relayed or repeated in a different form to confirm its accuracy.
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
Backups for important systems and data are tested at least annually.


Web Browser Management and DNS Filtering (BM)

Avoid danger and filter out connections to unauthorized websites, suspicious domains names, and known malicious domains names that will make you vulnerable to malware (such as ransomware) and phishing. Leverage DNS filtering, also known as DNS Blocking or DNS Firewall, with integrated threat intelligence. A number of effective commercial solutions are available ranging from free to low cost that make this an especially recommended action.
BM:B.Q01 Is malicious web content being blocked using DNS filtering via methods like DNS resolvers and DNS firewalls?

Question Intent: To determine if DNS filtering is being employed to block malicious web content.
Domain Name System (DNS) protection blocks dangerous sites and filters out unwanted content. A DNS look-up service works by translating a URL – a news site for example – to the actual IP address of that destination. Much like a phonebook that ties a person’s name to their phone number. DNS servers ensure devices are connecting through a secure portal. This adds a layer of protection against malware, phishing, and other viruses.
Possible actions:
Leverage DNS filtering, also known as DNS Blocking, DNS Firewall, or protective DNS, with integrated threat intelligence. DNS filtering is a method used to filter out undesirable and malicious content. A number of effective commercial solutions are available ranging from free to low cost. Filter out connections to unauthorized websites, suspicious domain names, and known malicious domain names associated with malware and phishing.
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
DNS filtering is actively employed by the organization to block malicious web content.
BM:B.Q02 Are web browser security settings managed?

Question Intent: To determine if web browser security settings are being managed by the organization.
Web browsers represent a common point of entry for malicious attack. Leverage email and web browser security settings to protect against spoofed or modified emails and unsecured webpages. Content filtering applied to external websites can prevent attackers from delivering malicious code to desktop applications. Firewalls can also deny traffic to potentially harmful sites while allowing access to acceptable applications. Updating the browsers security settings can make it much harder for malware to get installed. As an example, settings can be employed to reduce the ability of installing plugins and other add-ons and to disable certain types of content from automatically executing.
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
Browser security settings are actively employed and managed for the entire organization.

Phishing Prevention and Awareness (PP)

Take steps to increase and maintain awareness of Phishing threats. Conduct ongoing phishing and social engineering campaigns that randomly and periodically send simulated phishing emails to personnel. Offer phishing awareness training to staff which will assist in recognizing and reporting phishing attacks. Utilize an SMTP (mail server) proxy that employs reputational (IP, URL, and sender) and traditional anti-SPAM and content filtering features.
PP:B.Q01 Are annual tabletop exercises that include phishing response scenarios conducted?

Question Intent: To determine if the organization conducts tabletop exercises that include phishing response scenarios at least annually. Plan, prepare, and conduct drills for cyber-attacks, like phishing, and incidents as you would a fire or robbery. This involves having incident response plans and procedures, trained staff, assigned roles and responsibilities, and incident communications plans. A tabletop exercise is a discussion-based walk through of the incident response plan based on a simulated emergency described in a particular threat scenario. Team members will discuss their roles and response to a particular situation or incident that is described in the scenario. This helps the organization identify flaws or gaps in the organization's response and helps train and better prepare team members to respond in a real situation.
Possible actions:
Ensure that incident response plans account for cyber-attacks, like phishing and ransomware. Conduct drills such as tabletop exercises and ensure those exercises include phishing response scenarios.
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
Tabletop exercises that test the organizations incident response plan and include phishing response scenarios are conducted at least annually.
PP:B.Q02 Are users trained to recognize cyber threats like phishing?

Question Intent: To determine if all staff are trained to recognize cyber threats like Phishing attacks. Your staff is often the first line of defense for your organization. Investing in your personnel reduces vulnerabilities and drives a culture of ownership. They must be equipped to recognize cybersecurity risks such as phishing scams, password hacks, and outdated anti-malware, as well as trained to respond and share information appropriately. Regular training helps employees understand their role in cybersecurity, regardless of technical expertise, and the actions they take help keep your organization and customers secure. Training your staff and promoting cyber awareness does not mean you have to create training materials from scratch. Many professional organizations, industry associations and academic institutions, as well as private sector and government sources, provide ready-to-use cybersecurity training resources at no cost. Training should focus on threats employees encounter, like phishing emails, suspicious events to watch for, and simple best practices individual employees can adopt to reduce risk. Each aware employee strengthens your organization against attack, and act as another “sensor” to identify an attack.
Possible actions:
Identify available training resources through professional associations, academic institutions, private sector, and government sources. Train personnel about risks like phishing and business email compromise. Deliver regular updates and reminders to keep everyone aware of current threats and how to handle them if encountered. Ensure employees know how and to whom to report suspicious emails, possible phishing attempts and other anomalous activity.
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
All personnel are trained to recognize cyber threats like phishing through awareness training and other means.
PP:B.Q03 Is email filtered to protect against malicious content?

Question Intent: To determine if all email is filtered to protect against malicious content. Web browsers and email clients are very common points of entry for attackers. Emails can be crafted to spoof users into disclosing sensitive information. Using email filtering and malware scanning at the email gateway reduces the number of malicious emails reaching the organizations staff. Outbound email can also be filtered to prevent staff from inadvertently sending malicious email and to alert the organization that there may be an issue internally.
Possible Actions:
Leverage email and web browser security settings to protect against spoofed or modified emails and unsecured webpages. Employ spam protection mechanisms to detect and act on unsolicited messages. Spam can be transported by different means, including email, email attachments, and web accesses. Customize your email settings to allow safe mail. Set the content filters to send mail containing certain words and email addresses to the spam folder and to block malicious attachments.
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
All email received by the organization is filtered to block malicious content.

Network Perimeter Monitoring (NM)

Look for suspicious activity and react faster. Monitor internet traffic into and out of your organization. Be sure to use a product or service with integrated threat intelligence and consider subscribing to additional indicator sharing feeds, such as the Automated Indicator Sharing service provided by DHS, to automatically identify attacks and known bad actors.
NM:B.Q01 Is perimeter network traffic monitored?

Question Intent: To determine if perimeter network traffic is being actively monitored by the organization.
Manage perimeter network components and monitor internet traffic into and out of your organization. Establish a monitoring strategy to identify unusual activity that could indicate an attack. A continuous monitoring capability should allow staff to be alerted and respond to security incidents more quickly.
Possible actions:
Deploy intrusion detection or intrusion prevention systems. Intrusion detection and intrusion prevention systems are examples of a technologies capable of scanning and analyzing internet traffic for malicious activity, reporting on that activity, and potentially taking action against it. Deploy network traffic analyzers. Network traffic analyzers can monitor both the internet traffic that crosses the network perimeter and all of the internal network communications by analyzing network traffic and flow records.
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
All perimeter network traffic is actively being monitored to identify malicious activity.
NM:I.Q02 Is internal network traffic monitored?

Question Intent: To determine if the internal network traffic is being actively monitored by the organization.
Learn what is happening on your network. Manage internal network components, host and device components, data at rest and in transit, and user behavior and activities. Actively maintaining this information will give you a baseline for security testing and making security-based decisions. A continuous monitoring capability should increase the speed of detection and response and allow staff to be alerted and respond more quickly to security incidents.
Possible Actions:
Deploy network traffic analyzers. Network traffic analyzers can monitor all the internal network communications by analyzing network traffic and flow records as well as the internet traffic that crosses the network perimeter. Deploy network-based intrusion detection and/or intrusion prevention devices where appropriate. Deploy host-based intrusion prevention solutions where appropriate. Collect and evaluate network traffic flow logs.
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
Where appropriate, internal network traffic is being actively monitored by the organization.
NM:I.Q03 Are networks segmented to protect mission critical assets?

Question Intent: To determine if networks are segmented to protect the organization's mission critical assets.
Segmentation divides a computer network into smaller parts. Segmenting a network will improve network performance and security by reducing the attack surface and limiting how far an attack can spread. Network traffic can then be controlled between the various parts of the network. A segmentation policy can be implemented to limit the flow of network traffic by type, source address, and destination address to name a few.
Some technologies that can be used to implement a segmentation policy include: firewalls access control list virtual local area networks (VLANs) software defined networks and software defined access technology
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
Network segmentation has been implemented where appropriate to protect the organization’s mission critical assets.
NM:A.Q04 Has the organization established a baseline of network traffic and is it used to identify anomalous activity?

Question Intent: To determine if a baseline of network traffic has been established and is it actively being used to identify anomalous activity.
Learn what is happening on your network. Manage network and perimeter components, host and device components, data at rest and in transit, and user behavior and activities. Network traffic monitoring can provide organizations visibility into their networks and enable the organization to build a baseline of normal behavior over time. This baseline of information will assist the organization in making security-based decisions, finding and tracking malicious behavior, and in responding to incidents. It will also assist the organization in assessing network performance issues and application issues. Correlating network and system monitoring tools and mechanisms that typically work in isolation—including malicious code protection software, host monitoring, and network monitoring—can provide an organization-wide monitoring view and may reveal otherwise unseen attack patterns.
Possible Actions:
Deploy network traffic analyzers. Network traffic analyzers can monitor all the internal network communications by analyzing network traffic and flow records as well as the internet traffic that crosses the network perimeter. Collect network traffic flow logs and create network flow diagrams. Deploy host-based intrusion prevention solutions where appropriate.
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
The organization has established a baseline of network traffic and actively uses that baseline to try and identify anomalous activity.

Asset Management (AM)

Protecting your systems requires knowing which devices are connected to your network, which applications are in use, who has access to them, and the security measures in place. A cyber-ready business keeps its systems up-to-date and secure. These actions can support a proactive risk management culture and limit the risk of compromise. Remove unsupported or unauthorized hardware and software. Supported hardware and software generally allow you to receive updates and patches for vulnerabilities that otherwise are not available for unauthorized and unsupported assets. Inventory authorized hardware and software throughout your organization. Know the physical location and user of the hardware to keep patching updates current. This also allows for any unauthorized hardware or software to be identified and removed.
AM:B.Q01 Have the organization’s hardware and software assets been inventoried and is the inventory managed?

Question Intent: To determine if the organization’s hardware and software assets are inventoried and if that inventory is managed and maintained.
The organization should inventory its hardware and software assets Inventories of assets may exist in multiple forms or physical locations. Possible Actions:
Learn what is on your network:
Inventory all hardware and software assets so you know what is in-play and at-risk from attack. Ensure each asset has a complete asset profile. Notate and correct discrepancies between the current inventory and the documented inventory.
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
The organization inventories all hardware and software assets And, the inventory is managed and maintained.
AM:B.Q02 Has the organization removed all unsupported hardware and software from its operating environment?

Question Intent: To determine if the organization has identified and removed all unsupported hardware and software from its operating environment. Remove unsupported hardware and software. Supported hardware and software generally allow you to receive updates and patches for vulnerabilities that otherwise are not available for unsupported assets. If hardware or software is unsupported, yet necessary for the fulfillment of the enterprise’s mission, document an exception detailing mitigating controls and residual risk acceptance. For any unsupported hardware or software without an exception documentation, designate as unauthorized. Review the hardware and software list to periodically verify support. Operational environments may present unique requirements. The security challenges facing Industrial Control Systems (ICS) will require additional attention and consideration. For example, many utilize proprietary protocols and may not have the ability to support security software that is commonly used in a traditional Information Technology (IT) environment. ICS network architectures typically differ from traditional IT environments and may require the organization to employ compensating controls. Applying security controls to ICS often rely on segmentation and boundary control between the IT and OT domains, and careful control design around local and remote connectivity to reduce attack vectors used to gain access to ICS networks.
Possible Actions:
Inventory authorized hardware and software throughout your organization. Know the physical location and user of the hardware and software to keep patching updates current. This also allows for any unauthorized hardware or software to be identified and removed. Using the inventory, determine and remove all unsupported hardware or software. Other considerations for ICS: Obtain a list of recommended and supported hardware and software directly from the ICS manufactures and vendors. Evaluate removing unsupported hardware and software. If problematic evaluate adding compensating controls to protect the environment. Track end of life and end of support notices and ensure cybersecurity requirements are considered as part of any future sourcing and procurement processes.
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
The organization identifies and, where appropriate, removes all unsupported hardware and software from its operating environment.
AM:I.Q03 Does the organization detect rogue hardware and alert key stakeholders?

Question Intent: To determine if the organization implements a process to detect unauthorized (rogue) hardware and if detected, does the organization alert key stakeholders.
Monitor information systems to detect unauthorized or rogue hardware in the organization’s operating environment. Comparing against the organization's inventory can assist with this process. The organization should establish communication channels with key stakeholders that will receive these notifications. The organization should update service level agreements with key stakeholders if necessary to reflect commitment to notifications.
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
The organization actively detects rogue (unauthorized) hardware and alerts key stakeholders upon detection.
AM:A.Q04 Does the organization quarantine and/or remove all rogue hardware?

Question Intent: To determine if the organization removes unauthorized or rogue hardware from the operating environment.
Periodically identify the existence of rouge hardware through monitoring and auditing of network information systems and comparing against the organization’s inventory. Quarantine and/or remove all unauthorized (rogue) hardware from the operating environment.
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
Upon discovery, all rogue hardware is quarantined and ultimately removed from the organization’s network.
AM:B.Q05 Are documented and approved secure configurations used to manage the organization’s hardware and software assets?

Question Intent: To determine if documented and approved secure configurations are used to manage the organization’s hardware and software assets. Implement secure configurations for all hardware and software assets. This will help maintain the integrity of the assets by ensuring that they can be restored to an acceptable form when necessary (perhaps after a disruption). Create and maintain policies and processes that identify and prioritize secure configurations. Review and implement secure configuration guidance from your vendors and other sources. Configuration management is a process for managing the integrity of a technology asset over its lifetime. The resilience of critical services and the assets that support the service may be affected when the integrity of those assets is compromised. Configuration Management activities can include: determining which assets to place under configuration management identifying the configuration of selected assets creating configuration baselines controlling changes to configuration items maintaining the integrity of baseline configurations auditing configuration baselines
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
The organization creates and uses documented and approved secure configurations to manage its hardware and software assets.
AM:I.Q06 Are standard baseline images used to control hardware and software configurations?

Question Intent: To determine whether the organization has established configuration baseline images for hardware and software assets and if those baselines are used to control hardware and software asset configurations. Establishing a technology asset baseline (commonly called a configuration item) provides a foundation for managing the integrity of the asset as it changes over its lifecycle. Configuration management establishes additional controls over the configuration baseline so that the asset integrity is maintained and always in a form that is available and authorized for use.
Example configuration items:
software and application code
operating systems
hardware configuration files
firewall rulesets
configuration files for routers, switches, and other network equipment
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
Configuration baseline images exists for hardware and software assets and those baselines are used to control hardware and software asset configurations.
AM:A.Q07 Does the organization manage system configurations using security hardening guides?

Question Intent: To determine if security hardening guides are used to manage system configurations. Security hardening guides (also referred to as a security configuration checklist, lockdown guide, security guide, security technical implementation guide [STIG], or benchmark) are documents that: include instructions or procedures for configuring an IT product to an operational environment verify that the product has been configured properly and/or help identify unauthorized configuration changes to the product. The IT product may be commercial, open source, government-off-the-shelf (GOTS), etc.
Possible Actions: Using well-written, standardized configuration checklists can reduce the vulnerability exposure of IT products and be particularly helpful to small organizations and individuals in securing their systems. Checklists can be developed not only by IT vendors, but also by other organizations with technical competence in IT product security.
Security hardening guides might include any of the following: Configuration files that automatically set or verify various security-related settings (e.g., executables, security templates that modify settings, Security Content Automation Protocol (SCAP) XML (Extensible Markup Language) files, and scripts). Documentation (e.g., text file) that guides the checklist user to manually configure an IT product Documents that explain the recommended methods to securely install and configure a device Policy and programmatic documents that set forth guidelines for such things as auditing, authentication mechanisms (e.g., passwords), and perimeter security
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
Security hardening guides (or security configuration checklists, lockdown guides, security guides, security technical implementation guides [STIGs], or benchmarks) are used to manage the organization’s system configurations.

Patch And Update Management (PM)

Make it generally harder for the bad guys by eliminating known vulnerabilities. Obtain, test, and deploy software and firmware patches as quickly as practical. Enable automatic updates whenever possible. Replace end of life (EOL) / unsupported operating systems, applications, and hardware with vendor supported versions/models. granted the minimum privileges needed. Identify and deactivate unused accounts, eliminate shared accounts, remove unnecessary privileges, and enforce strong password policies. Monitor and analyze user activities for anomalous behavior such as access attempts outside of normal operating hours or from unusual locations. Consider mechanisms that are stronger than password authentication such as biometrics, one-time passwords and tokens for sensitive applications and functions. Multi-factor authentication, in particular, is highly recommended starting with privileged users first, then expanding to all users. User and Access Management will be a daunting and complex activity—and there is no "one size fits all" solution. Adopt a strategy appropriate to your organization and leverage a staged approach.
PM:B.Q01 Is all public-facing software patched for vulnerabilities within 15 days for vulnerabilities rated as “Critical” and 30 days for vulnerabilities rated as “High”?

Question Intent: To determine if all public facing systems are patched within the prescribed timeframe based on guidance and rating (15 days for “Critical” and 30 days for “High”) What are patches? Patches are software and operating system (OS) updates that address security vulnerabilities within a program or product. Software vendors may choose to release updates to fix performance bugs, as well as to provide enhanced security features. When possible, leverage automatic updates for all operating systems and third-party software. An easy step is to establish and maintain network security/patching procedures to prevent attacks by configuring functions and programs necessary for security. Be sure to obtain, test, and deploy the latest versions of operating systems and applications. Enabling automatic updates should be done with care. Take the time to determine and establish an appropriately architected patching infrastructure and process, and ensure it meets the business need. For example, a centralized solution may be best as you do not want every system on the network obtaining patches individually from the internet. Operational environments may present unique requirements. The security challenges facing Industrial Control Systems (ICS) will require additional attention and consideration. It may not be possible to employ automated patching methods to all ICS environment assets. It is very important that vendor patches be tested in a test environment that closely mimics the production environment prior to deploying the patch to the production environment.
A possible software patching process:
1. Plan
Use the organization's inventory of hardware and software assets to help determine which patches need to be applied and to help ensure that all applications and devices are kept patched. Consider using the inventory of assets to group assets by the level of risk and impact they would pose to the organization if they were disrupted. Consider using the inventory to streamline and consolidate the number of software versions in the environment. This will reduce the complexity and overhead of the patching process. Establish sources of vendor patching information and track vendor security update announcements.
2. Analyze and Test
Analyze the effects of the patch in your environment and determine if you will encounter exceptions where a patch can’t be deployed immediately. Until the patch can be applied, consider the types of compensating controls you can put in place to protect those assets. Establish a test environment and if possible, test the patch before it is deployed to the entire environment. Expect bumps in the road. Develop a plan for rolling out the patch. If the testing was successful, you may consider planning to roll out the patch in a phased approach. Patch a small set of production systems to evaluate the effects before rolling it out to the rest of the environment. You may also consider multiple phases in your approach. A backout plan should also be developed in case there are issues.
3. Implement
Use the organization’s change control process to get approval to implement the patch. Much of the information collected above will need to be part of the change request. Consider backing up the appropriate assets prior to pushing out the patch. Once the change is approved, patch the appropriate assets according to the plan. Closely monitor critical assets for issues and implement the backout plan if necessary.
4. Review
Go back and review all the systems that were patched and verify that the patch was applied. It is not unusual to find that the patch was unsuccessful on a small percentage of systems Reapply the patch to the systems that the patch was unsuccessful for. Use any lessons learned to improve the patch process.
Possible considerations:
Enable automatic software updates whenever possible. This will ensure that software updates are installed as quickly as possible. When possible, do not use unsupported end of life (EOL) software. Always visit vendor sites directly rather than clicking on advertisements or email links. Avoid software updates while using untrusted networks. New vulnerabilities are continually emerging, but the best defense against attackers exploiting patched vulnerabilities is simple: keep your software up to date. This is one of the most effective measures you can take to protect your computer, phone, and other digital devices. Vulnerabilities are based on the Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures (CVE) vulnerability naming standard and are organized according to severity, determined by the Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS) standard. The division of high, medium, and low severities correspond to the following scores: High: vulnerabilities with a CVSS base score of 7.0–10.0 Medium: vulnerabilities with a CVSS base score of 4.0–6.9 Low: vulnerabilities with a CVSS base score of 0.0–3.9
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
All public facing software with vulnerabilities rated “High” are patched within 30 days. And, all public facing software with vulnerabilities rated as “Critical” are patched within 15 days.

UM:B.Q01 Are strong and unique passwords implemented throughout the entire organization?

Question Intent: To determine if all accounts require strong and unique passwords. Passwords are the most common means of authentication, but only work if they are complex and confidential. Although intentionally misspelling a word ("daytt" instead of "date") may offer some protection against specific types of attacks (e.g. dictionary attacks) an even better method is to rely on a series of words and use memory techniques, or mnemonics, to help you remember how to decode it. Some hackers are more sophisticated and use algorithms to figure out passwords, so consider mechanisms that are stronger than password authentication such as biometrics, one-time passwords, and tokens for sensitive applications and functions. The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) has developed specific guidelines for strong passwords. According to NIST guidance, you should consider using the longest password or passphrase permissible (8–64 characters) when you can. For example, "Pattern2baseball#4mYmiemale!" would be a strong password because it has 28 characters and includes the upper and lowercase letters, numbers, and special characters.
Possible actions:

Question Intent: To determine if the principle of least privilege is enforced through policies and procedure to control user access. Least privilege is the restriction of privileges to only those accounts that require them to perform their duties, while limiting accounts to only those privileges that are truly necessary. Doing this reduces the exposure of those privileges to a smaller, more easily manageable set of accounts. Local administrative accounts and accounts for software program management and installation are particularly powerful, but have small scopes of control and should be restricted as much as possible. IR:I.Q06 Is a physical incident response exercise performed at least once a year?

To determine if the organization performs physical incident response exercises at least once a year.
Incident response testing should progress in stages, such as:
plan review
tabletop test or walkthrough
component testing, such as incident declaration and team notification
limited scenario testing, using an isolated incident or a single-category incident as in an IT failure
large-scale incident testing, using a regional disaster or pandemic and testing cross-plan dependencies
By testing your incident response plan, you allow your organization and the personnel directly involved in managing incidents the opportunity to practice roles and responsibilities in a controlled environment. You also ensure that the plan produces the desired outcomes when tested. Incident management exercises are not meant to result in pass/fail “grades.” Instead, they should be viewed as opportunities to measure plan performance and to expose any unexpected outcomes, conflicts, and needed improvements in a controlled environment.
Possible Actions:
Guidelines and standards for incident management plan exercises can include the following:
Identification and responsibilities of plan owners
Identification and involvement of stakeholders
Exercise management
Plan exercise schedule
Requirements for what information must be documented during and after a plan exercise
Measures for how the organization evaluates incident management plan performance
Exercise plans
Test plans developed using pre-established exercise criteria
Plan exercise results
Define acceptable response times and process adherence in accordance with the plan
Strategy for testing and maintaining the incident management
Establish guidelines for plan testing; using lessons learned to improve the incident management plan
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
The organization performs a physical incident response exercise at least once a year.

IR:I.Q08 Has the organization implemented redundant systems where appropriate for the purpose of resiliency?

Question Intent :
To determine if the organization has implemented redundant systems where appropriate for the purpose of resiliency.
Availability. The system’s availability (i.e., reliability) requirements are an important factor in design. Systems with strong availability/up-time requirements may require more redundancy.
Maintaining functionality during adverse conditions. This involves designing the industrial control systems (ICS) so that each critical component has a redundant counterpart. Additionally, if a component fails, it should fail in a manner that does not generate unnecessary traffic on the ICS or other networks, or does not cause another problem elsewhere, such as a cascading event. The ICS should also allow for graceful degradation such as moving from "normal operation" with full automation to "emergency operation" with operators more involved and less automation to "manual operation" with no automation.
The organization must plan to sustain technology assets to ensure the continued operation of services. Mechanisms implemented to achieve this practice can take many forms:
1.load balancing : a method that improves the distribution of workloads across multiple resources to optimize resource use, maximize throughput, minimize response time, and avoid overload of any single resource
2.hot swap capabilities: the ability to replace or add components without stopping or shutting down the associated system
3.high availability implementations: a failover methodology to ensure availability during device or component interruptions
4.alternate telecommunications services
5.alternate processing sites
6.fail-safe procedures:
6.1fail-safe: a design feature or practice that, in the event of a specific type of failure, inherently responds in a way that will cause no or minimal harm to system assets, property, or life
6.2Fail-safe procedures may include alerting operator personnel and providing specific instructions on subsequent steps to take (e.g., do nothing, reestablish system settings, shut down processes, restart the system, or contact designated organizational personnel).
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
The organization has implemented redundant systems where appropriate for the purpose of resiliency.

Risk Management (RM)

Lead investment in cybersecurity by investing in capabilities for your organization and staff. This includes not only investments in technological capabilities, but also a continuous investment in cybersecurity training and awareness capabilities for your organization’s personnel. Have conversations with your staff, business partners, vendors, managed service providers, and others within your supply chain. RM:I.Q01 Does the organization perform business impact assessments?

Question Intent :
To determine if the organization performs business impact assessments as part of its service continuity program.
The Business Impact Analysis (BIA) is a foundational element of a service continuity program. The BIA measures the effects of a disruption on an organization’s services and how they impact changes over time.
Leverage business impact assessments to prioritize resources and identify which systems must be recovered first. Business impact analysis helps identify and prioritize critical systems, information, and assets. This information determines contingency requirements and priorities for critical information and services. It also allows planning for disruption impacts and identifies allowable outage times. This enables personnel to develop and prioritize recovery strategies that can be used.
Possible Actions:
Define business impact analysis characteristics.
The BIA considers the impact of disruptions over time and provides requirements for the development of continuity plans. Consider the best time frames for capturing impact, for example, four hours, one day, two days, or even days, weeks, or months.
The BIA is the key process tool used to establish continuity requirements and prioritize business services’ importance to meeting an organization’s mission. Consider the requirements that should be collected—for example, resources required and their relative importance.
The BIA identifies what to recover and when that recovery needs to be completed. Consider which services must be up and running and when.
Establish a link to risk assessments. Risk assessments help identify the key exposures to consider in the BIA by providing insights into the likelihood and potential impact of various disruption threats, whether natural or man-made. Leverage existing risk assessments that the organization may have conducted to
obtain input to the BIA and integrate with evaluation and mitigation activities already underway. Typically, groups involved in operational risk, enterprise risk, finance, audits, or compliance are good sources of information on previous risk assessments.
identify the essential services of the organization. Previous risk assessment documentation or senior leaders can be consulted when establishing this list.
determine the priority of services that are most essential or that need to get back online fastest in the event of a disruption. Service owners, managers, and senior leadership are good sources for establishing priorities.
Determine how to measure the impact of a service disruption. Impact information is usually measured in dollars, customers affected, reputational impact, and regulatory noncompliance. These measures of impact can inform the development of continuity requirements and strategies. In many instances, organizations look to the BIA information to develop a business case for investment in mitigation activities. This strategy aspect of continuity planning is a core driver of continuity process management. Organizations doing continuity planning must weigh spending and investment against risk. The continuity strategy decision process can clarify the risk tolerance of the organization, for example, a willingness to accept the risk of longer recovery times to keep costs down. See CRR Resource Guide Service Continuity Management v1.1, Appendix A, Business Impact Analysis Template.
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
The organization performs business impact assessments to identify critical business services and assets.
RM:A.Q02 Has the organization defined organizational risk criteria and tolerances?

Question Intent :
To determine if the organization has defined risk criteria and if risk tolerances thresholds have been defined for each criteria. Organizational risk assessments are used, in part, to inform the security and privacy control selection process. The selection process results in an agreed-upon set of security and privacy controls addressing specific mission or business needs consistent with organizational risk tolerance. Risk framing is most effective when conducted at the organization level and in consultation with stakeholders throughout the organization including mission, business, and system owners. The assumptions, constraints, risk tolerance, priorities, and trade-offs identified as part of the risk framing process inform the risk management strategy, which in turn informs the conduct of risk assessment, risk response, and risk monitoring activities. Risk tolerance is defined as the level of risk or the degree of uncertainty that is acceptable to an organization. Risk parameters may differ for each impact area. Risk tolerance parameters describe the risk measurement criteria for each impact area. Risk measurement criteria include how impact is measured, how frequently it is measured, and what is to be measured. Risk tolerance thresholds, which trigger action, should be defined for each category of risk.
Risk tolerance parameters describe the risk measurement criteria for each impact area.
Risk tolerance thresholds are used by management to determine when a risk is in control or when it has exceeded acceptable limits.
Risk tolerance thresholds should be set for each category/parameter of risk that the organization establishes as a means for measuring and managing risk. For example:
A risk tolerance threshold for a category of risk may be whenever more than 200 users are impacted.
The risk tolerance threshold indicates when action needs to be taken to prevent operational disruption.
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
The organization has defined risk criteria and risk tolerance thresholds for each criteria of risk.
RM:A.Q03 Does the organization consider risk inheritance and exposure between its various interconnected systems?

Question Intent:
To determine whether the organization considers risk inheritance and exposure between its various interconnected systems. Organizations should consider the risk related to new or increased threats that may be introduced when systems exchange information with other systems that may have different security and privacy requirements and controls. This includes systems within the same organization and systems that are external to the organization.
Risk assessments can support a wide variety of risk-based decisions and activities including the definition of interconnection security requirements for information exchange. System information exchange security requirements apply to information exchanges between two or more systems. Examples include:
1.System information exchanges include connections via leased lines
2.virtual private networks
3.connections to internet service providers
4.connections and exchanges with cloud services
5.exchanges via web-based services
6.exchanges of files via file transfer protocol
7.other organization-to-organization communications.
Possible Actions:
Identify transitive (downstream) information exchanges with other systems Verify that individuals or systems transferring data between interconnecting systems have the requisite authorizations (i.e., write permissions or privileges) prior to accepting such data. Take measures to ensure that transitive (downstream) information exchanges cease when the controls on identified transitive (downstream) systems cannot be verified or validated. Approve and manage the exchange of information between the systems using: interconnection security agreements information exchange security agreements memoranda of understanding or agreement service level agreements Document, as part of each exchange agreement, the interface characteristics, security and privacy requirements, controls, and responsibilities for each system, and the impact level of the information communicated; and Periodically review and update the agreements as necessary.
Criteria for “Yes” Response:
The organization considers risk inheritance and exposure between all of its interconnected systems.
RM:A.Q04 Does the organization apply quantitative risk analysis to remediation activities?

Question Intent:
To determine if the organization applies meaningful and measurable quantitative risk analysis to its remediation activities, which when acted upon, directly reduces the risk to the organization. To effectively analyze metrics related to remediation activities, the organization should assess the risk management plan against its objectives using quantitative risk analysis. When risks appear to be exceeding the organization’s tolerances, action needs to be taken. Determining when risks should be addressed is often complex and hard to measure. Organizations may find that the best approach is to use a combination of metrics that are collaboratively reviewed to arrive at actionable decisions.
In applying quantitative measurements to risk analysis, it can be useful to first understand the difference between quantitative and qualitative approaches:
Qualitative measurements:
typically aimed at collecting and interpreting empirical data that cannot be expressed in numbers (e.g., internal or external audit ratings on overall results: good, fair, poor).
Quantitative measurements:
more formalized measures that consider both impact and probabilities:
Quantitative measure examples:
technical measures (e.g., availability, virus infection rates, problem reports, access violations) incident and service continuity plan tested as scheduled risk policy/standards exception and violation rates realized risk impacts in dollars, customers, reputation, stock price, etc. Useful / Less Useful Metrics To illustrate the difference between a meaningful and measurable cybersecurity risk metric and one that is less useful, consider the following: Useful metric: the time it takes an organization to patch a critical vulnerability across the enterprise. In this example, reducing the days it takes to patch a vulnerability directly reduces the risk to the organization. Less useful metric: the number of alerts a Security Operations Center (SOC) receives in a week. There are too many variables in the number of alerts an SOC receives for this number to be consistently relevant.
Possible Actions:
Develop a clear understanding of the most important risks the organization is facing by prioritizing the risks. Prioritize based on impact to the organization if the risk is realized. Develop reporting and quantitative risk metrics designed to measure the effectiveness of the remediation activities. Establish a schedule and format for reporting on these risks. Periodically report on these risks and related metrics to stakeholders, including leaders and operational managers. Gather feedback from the reporting activity to improve the risk metrics and the reporting activity. Criteria for “Yes” Response: The organization applies quantitative risk analysis to its remediation activities.
